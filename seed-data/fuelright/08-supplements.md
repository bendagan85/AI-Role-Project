# Supplements — Evidence Tiers

General performance nutrition guidance. Not medical advice. If you take medications or have conditions, check supplement interactions with a clinician.

## How to think about supplements

Supplements are at most a small percentage of your total result. They live on top of: training, sleep, total calories, total protein, hydration, and consistency. None of those can be substituted with a pill.

That said, a few supplements have strong evidence behind them. The rest are noise, marketing, or expensive urine.

## Tier 1: Strong evidence, worth using

### Creatine monohydrate
- 3–5g/day, every day, not just on training days
- Best-researched supplement in sports nutrition
- Increases strength output by ~5%, supports muscle growth, modest cognitive benefits
- Causes 1–2 kg of water weight retention (in the muscle — not bloating)
- Doesn't damage kidneys in healthy adults despite the urban legend
- Cheap — bulk powder is ~$15 for 3 months

### Caffeine
- 3–6 mg/kg before training (200–450 mg for most adults)
- One of the most-researched ergogenic aids
- Improves strength output, endurance, focus
- Build tolerance over time — cycle off periodically

### Protein powder
- Not a "supplement" in the magical sense — it's just protein
- Useful for hitting daily targets
- Whey is the gold standard; casein for slower release; plant blends for vegans

### Vitamin D
- If you live above 35° latitude, don't get much sun, or have dark skin
- 1000–4000 IU per day depending on baseline status
- Get a blood test if unsure (target 75–125 nmol/L)
- Most people are deficient and don't know it

### Omega-3 fish oil (EPA + DHA)
- 1–3g of combined EPA+DHA per day
- Useful if you don't eat fatty fish 1–2x per week
- Anti-inflammatory, cardiovascular, brain health benefits

## Tier 2: Decent evidence, situational

### Beta-alanine
- 3–6g/day for ~4 weeks to load
- Helps with reps 8–15 (slow oxidative work) more than 1–5 rep sets
- Causes tingling at high doses (harmless)

### Citrulline malate
- 6–8g pre-workout
- Modest pump and endurance benefits
- Often included in pre-workouts

### Electrolytes / sodium
- If you sweat heavily, are on a low-sodium diet, or train in heat
- Plain table salt works for sodium; broader electrolyte mixes for full coverage

### Magnesium
- 300–400 mg/day if your diet is low in nuts, leafy greens, legumes
- Magnesium glycinate is well-absorbed and easier on the gut

## Tier 3: Weak or mixed evidence

These have some studies suggesting benefit but mixed or modest results:
- BCAAs (redundant if you hit protein targets)
- Glutamine
- Beta-hydroxy-beta-methylbutyrate (HMB)
- Ashwagandha (some evidence for cortisol modulation, sleep)
- Curcumin (anti-inflammatory)

These aren't necessarily wastes of money if you have specific reasons to use them. But they're optional.

## Tier 4: Skip

- Testosterone boosters
- Fat burners
- "Pump" products with proprietary blends
- Most "anabolic" pills
- Anything that promises results comparable to anabolic steroids while being legal

## Practical stack for most lifters

If I had to recommend the basic stack for a serious trainee:
1. Creatine monohydrate, 5g daily
2. Whey protein, 1–2 scoops daily as needed for protein targets
3. Caffeine, pre-training (or just coffee/tea)
4. Vitamin D, 2000 IU daily (if low-sun)
5. Fish oil, 2g EPA+DHA (if you don't eat fish 2x/week)

Total monthly cost: under $50 for most people, often under $30. Anyone selling you a stack that costs more without these basics in it is selling you marketing.

## What about the new stuff?

Every year, new "breakthrough" supplements appear. Almost none survive 5 years of follow-up studies. Wait for replication. The Tier 1 list above has been stable for over a decade — that's a feature, not a bug.

## When to talk to a doctor

- Before adding supplements if you take prescription medications
- If you have liver, kidney, or heart conditions
- If you're pregnant or nursing
- If you experience side effects from any supplement
