# Protein Requirements for Active Adults

Standard disclaimer: I'm a dietitian giving general performance nutrition guidance, not medical advice for your specific situation.

## The headline numbers

For most active, healthy adults:

- **General active population:** 1.4–1.8 g per kg of bodyweight per day.
- **Strength athletes (most lifters):** 1.6–2.2 g/kg/day.
- **Strength athletes in a calorie deficit (cutting):** 2.0–2.4 g/kg/day.
- **Older adults (50+) training resistance:** 1.6–2.0 g/kg/day, lean toward the higher end.

These ranges are based on multiple meta-analyses of nitrogen balance and muscle protein synthesis studies. The classic 0.8 g/kg "RDA" is for sedentary populations and is way too low for anyone training seriously.

## Why protein matters more during a cut

When you're in a calorie deficit, your body breaks down body tissue for fuel. Some of that will be fat (the goal) and some will be muscle (not the goal). Higher protein intake protects muscle:
- More substrate available for repair
- Higher satiety, helping adherence
- Higher TEF, modestly increasing energy expenditure

## How to distribute it

Total daily intake matters most. WITHIN that, evenly-distributed protein across 3–5 meals supports muscle protein synthesis better than 1–2 huge meals.

Aim for **30–50g of protein per meal**, spaced ~3–5 hours apart. Most adults respond well to 4 meals of 35–45g.

The bigger you are (or the more muscle mass you have), the higher per-meal dose you can effectively use. Larger adults benefit from the upper end of the range.

## Protein sources

Per ~25g of protein (a typical serving for one meal contribution):

- Chicken breast: 100g
- Greek yogurt (2%): 250g
- Whey protein powder: 1 scoop (~30g powder)
- Eggs: 3.5 large eggs
- Lentils: 3/4 cup cooked (with less leucine — combine with grains)
- Tofu: ~200g
- Salmon: ~120g

If you don't eat meat, you'll lean more on dairy, eggs, legumes, and whey or plant-based protein powders. Mixed plant sources (lentils + rice, tofu + grains) cover the full amino acid spectrum.

## Is too much protein dangerous?

For healthy kidneys, no. Decades of research have not found harm from high protein intake (up to ~3 g/kg) in people without kidney disease. The "high protein damages kidneys" idea comes from observations in people WITH existing kidney disease — protein restriction is appropriate THEN.

If you have kidney disease or family history thereof, get specific medical guidance.

## When you don't need that much

- **Sedentary adults not trying to build muscle:** 0.8–1.2 g/kg is plenty.
- **Endurance-only athletes:** 1.2–1.6 g/kg covers it.
- **Older adults trying to maintain function without training:** 1.0–1.2 g/kg, ideally spread across the day.

## Practical example

A 75 kg lifter trying to gain muscle:
- 75 × 1.8 = 135g of protein per day
- 4 meals of ~33g each, or 3 meals of ~40g and a snack of 15g
- Real-food sources cover most of this; one daily whey shake fills any gap

## Note about protein powders

Whey protein is the most studied and most digestible. Casein is slower and useful before sleep. Plant-based blends (pea + rice) are fine and often equal whey on a per-gram basis if you bump the dose 10–15%.

You don't NEED protein powder. It's just a convenient way to hit your target. Real food first, supplement as needed.
