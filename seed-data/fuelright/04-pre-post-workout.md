# Pre-Workout and Post-Workout Nutrition

Reminder: general performance nutrition advice. Not medical advice.

## The "anabolic window" is mostly a myth

For decades, people believed you had to consume protein within 30 minutes of finishing a workout or "you'd waste the session." The research has updated. The window is much wider than 30 minutes — probably 3–4 hours on either side of training.

What this means in practice:
- Don't stress about post-workout timing if you ate within 2–3 hours before training.
- Don't skip post-workout food, but don't sprint to it either.
- Total daily protein and calories matter far more than the precise hour of intake.

## Pre-workout nutrition

The goals:
1. Adequate glycogen for the work coming
2. Avoid GI distress during training
3. Maintain stable blood sugar

For training within 2–3 hours of a normal meal: just eat normally. Plate balanced with protein + carbs + some fat.

For training closer to a meal (within 1 hour): keep it small and easy to digest. Reduce fat and fiber, lean into carbs and lean protein. Examples:
- Banana + Greek yogurt
- Toast with honey + a few eggs
- Oats with whey protein

For training fasted (early morning, before breakfast): glycogen is adequate for short or moderate sessions. Long or intense sessions will feel harder. Sip 20–30g of carbs (sport drink, banana) during long sessions if energy drops.

## Pre-workout caffeine

A solid ergogenic aid. 3–6 mg/kg of bodyweight, 30–45 minutes before training. For a 75 kg adult that's 225–450 mg.

Notes:
- Tolerance builds. Cycle off periodically (1 week off every 6–8 weeks).
- Effects diminish above 6 mg/kg.
- Don't take after 2 PM if you sleep at 10 PM.
- Coffee, tea, and supplements all count toward the total.

## Post-workout nutrition

Goals:
1. Replenish glycogen
2. Provide protein for repair
3. Hydrate

Aim for a meal within 2–3 hours containing:
- 30–50g of protein
- 0.5–1.0 g/kg of carbs
- Some fat (not zero — fat doesn't blunt protein synthesis meaningfully)

Whole-food meal is fine. Don't sprint to a shake — chicken, rice, vegetables works equally well.

## The exception: two-a-day training

If you're training twice in a day (e.g., AM strength, PM hypertrophy), then post-workout speed matters MORE for the first session, because glycogen replenishment is genuinely time-sensitive when you'll be working again in 6 hours.

In that case: 1.0 g/kg carbs + 30g protein within 60 minutes of the first session, then a normal meal 2–3 hours later, then your second session.

## Hydration around training

Pre: 500 mL water in the 1–2 hours before training.
During: sip throughout. For sessions under an hour, water is fine. For longer sessions, especially in heat, add electrolytes.
Post: 1.5x the fluid you sweated. Check by weighing yourself before and after — every 1 kg drop is 1 liter you should drink back.

## Sodium and long sessions

For sessions over 90 minutes or in hot environments, add ~500 mg of sodium per hour. Sports drinks, electrolyte powders, or even a pinch of salt in water with a banana.

Many lifters who feel "weak" or get cramps during long sessions are under-sodium, not under-water.

## What to skip

- Pre-workout supplements with mystery proprietary blends. Caffeine + creatine cover 90% of the ergogenic value, and you can take those separately.
- Branched-chain amino acid (BCAA) supplements. Redundant if you hit daily protein.
- "Anabolic windows" tighter than 2 hours post-workout. The science doesn't support that level of urgency.
