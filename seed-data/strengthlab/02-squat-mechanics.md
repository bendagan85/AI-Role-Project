# Squat Mechanics — Common Mistakes & Cues

The squat is the single most informative lift in your program. If your squat is moving up, your other lifts almost certainly are too. If it stalls, the diagnosis usually reveals broader issues — mobility, technique, or programming. Here's a working reference.

## Setup

- **Bar placement:** High-bar sits on top of the traps; low-bar sits across the rear delts. Pick one and stick with it for a training block — switching weekly muddies the data.
- **Stance:** Feet roughly shoulder-width apart, toes turned out 15–30°. The exact angle depends on hip anatomy — there is no single "correct" stance.
- **Grip:** Hands as close as you can without losing thoracic position. A tighter grip creates a more stable upper-back shelf.
- **Brace:** Big breath into the belly, tighten the abs as if bracing for a punch. Hold this throughout the rep.

## Descent

- Sit back AND down at the same time — neither pure squat-back (good morning) nor pure squat-down (knee dominant).
- Knees track over the toes; don't let them cave inward (valgus collapse).
- Maintain the upper-back arch and chest position.

## Depth target

Hip crease at or below the top of the knee. Anything shallower is leaving stimulus on the table and risks knee dominance.

## Ascent

- Drive the entire foot into the floor — heel and ball both.
- The chest leads. If your hips shoot up and you good-morning the rep, the weight is too heavy or your quads are weak.
- Exhale on the way up, maintain bracing.

## The six recurring mistakes

1. **Knee cave (valgus collapse).** Cue: "spread the floor apart" with your feet.
2. **Butt-wink at depth.** Usually a mobility issue. Address ankle dorsiflexion first.
3. **Hip shoot.** Hips rise faster than chest. Either too heavy or quads are weak relative to hips.
4. **Heel rise.** Ankle mobility, stance width too narrow, or sitting too far back.
5. **Forward bar drift.** The bar should travel straight up over the mid-foot. If it drifts forward, you'll dump load onto your lower back.
6. **Holding breath the whole set.** Brace AND breathe between reps for sets > 3. For singles, holding through the whole rep is fine.

## Programming notes

For strength: 3–5 reps at RPE 7–8 across 3–5 working sets, twice per week. For hypertrophy: 6–12 reps at RPE 8–9 with shorter rest. Always warm up with 4–6 progressively heavier sets before working weight.

## When something hurts

Sharp pain, especially lower back or knee, is a stop signal. Reduce the weight, video the lift, identify what's breaking down. Most squat injuries trace back to one of: weight too heavy, technique breakdown under fatigue, or a mobility limitation that was tolerable at light weights but isn't at heavy.
