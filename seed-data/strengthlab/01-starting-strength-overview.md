# Starting Strength — Overview

If you're new to lifting or coming back after a long break, a linear progression program is almost certainly your best path. The premise is simple: lift weights, eat enough, recover, then lift slightly more next session. For 8–16 weeks this works ridiculously well.

## The four lifts

A novice program is built around four compound movements:

1. **Back squat** — the foundational lower-body movement.
2. **Bench press** — the primary upper-body push.
3. **Deadlift** — total-body posterior chain.
4. **Overhead press** — vertical upper-body push.

These four cover the major movement patterns and recruit the most muscle for the time invested. Programs like Starting Strength, StrongLifts, and many introductory templates use this same skeleton.

## The progression rule

For your first 8–12 weeks, you add weight every session. Typical increments:
- Squat / Deadlift: +2.5–5 kg per session
- Bench / Overhead: +1–2.5 kg per session
- Once linear progression stalls twice on the same lift, you transition to a different progression style (intermediate programming, weekly progression, or block periodization).

## Typical session

Three full-body sessions per week, alternating two templates:

**Template A:** Squat 3x5, Bench 3x5, Deadlift 1x5
**Template B:** Squat 3x5, Overhead Press 3x5, Power Clean 5x3 (or barbell row)

Three workouts per week, ~45–60 minutes each, with a day of rest between. That's it.

## Why this works for novices

A novice's nervous system is so undertrained that even small increases in load drive measurable adaptation. The ceiling on linear progression is your recovery — when adding weight stops working twice in a row, you've outgrown the template.

The big mistake newbies make is program-hopping. Pick one template, follow it for 3 months, then evaluate. Switching every two weeks because you saw a YouTube video means you never give any program enough time to work.

## Eating to grow

Strength gain on a calorie deficit is possible but slow. For best results during a novice phase:
- Maintenance calories or +200–300 kcal/day surplus
- 1.6–2.2 g/kg of bodyweight in protein daily
- Plenty of carbs around training to support working hard in the gym

## When to stop the novice template

Move on when:
- Linear progression fails twice on the same lift even after a deload
- You've been training consistently for 4+ months
- Total tonnage per session is high enough that 3x5 leaves you wrecked

At that point look at intermediate templates like 5/3/1, Texas Method, or block periodization.
