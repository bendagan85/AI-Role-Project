# Deadlift — Form & Variations

The deadlift is the most honest lift. There's no place to hide bad position once the bar leaves the floor. A few non-negotiables and a few variations to know.

## Conventional setup (the default)

1. **Stance:** Mid-foot directly under the bar, feet hip-width apart. Toes forward or slightly turned out.
2. **Grip:** Hands just outside the legs. The bar should NOT shift forward when you grip — your shoulders are directly over (or just slightly in front of) the bar.
3. **Hinge to the bar:** Push the hips back, let the knees track forward to the bar. Don't squat down to the bar.
4. **Lats engaged:** Pull the bar back into your shins. "Bend the bar around your legs" is the cue.
5. **Brace:** Big breath, tighten the trunk, then pull.

## The pull

- The bar travels in a straight vertical line. If it loops out away from your body, your lats aren't engaged or your hips are too high.
- Lock out by squeezing glutes — don't lean back at the top.
- Reset between reps. Touch-and-go on the deadlift is a recipe for losing position.

## Conventional vs Sumo

Both are legitimate. The differences:

- **Conventional:** Narrower stance, more posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, low back). Greater range of motion.
- **Sumo:** Wide stance, more quad and adductor involvement. Shorter range of motion. Typically easier on the lower back.

Pick whichever feels stronger and more natural. Lifters with shorter arms and longer torsos often do better with sumo. Try both for a training block each before deciding.

## Romanian deadlift (RDL)

A hinge-only variation — knees stay mostly bent at the same angle throughout. The bar travels from hip height down to mid-shin, then back up. Targets hamstrings and glutes heavily. Great accessory for the conventional pull.

## Trap bar deadlift

Easier to learn, lower risk on the lower back. Knees can travel forward more, putting it somewhere between a deadlift and a squat. Good for beginners and athletes who don't compete in powerlifting.

## Common mistakes

1. **Hips too low at the start.** Your deadlift is not a squat. The bar should be over mid-foot with your shins almost vertical, not flexed forward.
2. **Hips shoot up.** If your hips rise faster than the bar at the start, your back is doing most of the work. Slow the start; drive with the legs first.
3. **Bar drifts forward.** Lats not engaged. Cue: "pull the bar into you."
4. **Rounded lower back.** Almost always a load issue, not a form one. Drop the weight, reset position.
5. **Hyperextending at the top.** Lock out is "stand tall" — not "lean back". Save your lower back.

## Frequency and volume

For strength, 1–2 heavy deadlift sessions per week is plenty. Deadlifts are taxing — most lifters need more recovery between deadlift sessions than between squat sessions. Volume comes from RDLs, block pulls, and other variations on alternate days.

## When to back off

If you ever feel your form break down on a deadlift, stop. Drop the weight 20–30%, finish your reps with clean form, and address whatever caused the breakdown. Deadlifting through poor form is how injuries happen.
