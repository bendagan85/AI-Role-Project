# Macronutrients — The Basics

Disclaimer up front: I'm not your doctor or your dietitian-of-record. This is general performance nutrition information. If you have a medical condition or are pregnant, talk to a qualified clinician.

## The three macros

There are three macronutrients that provide energy: protein, carbohydrates, and fat. Each has different roles, different caloric density, and different roles in performance and body composition.

| Macro | kcal per gram | Primary role |
|---|---|---|
| Protein | 4 | Tissue repair, muscle protein synthesis, satiety |
| Carbohydrate | 4 | Primary fuel for moderate-to-high-intensity training |
| Fat | 9 | Hormone production, fat-soluble vitamin absorption, longer-burn fuel |

Alcohol is a fourth caloric source (7 kcal/g) but it's not a macro and doesn't serve a useful nutritional function.

## How much of each

A reasonable starting point for an active adult:

- **Protein:** 1.6–2.2 g/kg of bodyweight (higher end for actively training to gain or recomp).
- **Fat:** 0.6–1.0 g/kg minimum. Going below 0.5 g/kg long-term can disrupt hormones.
- **Carbs:** the rest of your calories. For active people that's typically 3–6 g/kg.

These are starting points. Individual response varies.

## Calorie targets

Calories matter more than macro ratios for body composition. To lose fat, you need a deficit. To gain muscle, you need at minimum maintenance, and a small surplus is usually best.

That doesn't mean macros don't matter — given the SAME calorie intake, the macro split affects:
- How you feel (energy levels, satiety)
- How your training quality holds up
- How much of your weight change is muscle vs fat

## Protein quality

All protein is made from the same 20 amino acids, but sources vary in:
- **Digestibility** — animal protein is generally more digestible than plant.
- **Leucine content** — leucine triggers muscle protein synthesis. Whey is highest per gram.
- **Co-nutrient profile** — fish gives you omega-3s, dairy gives you calcium, etc.

Practical implication: if you're hitting your total daily protein target with a varied diet, quality isn't a problem. If you're vegan, you may want to slightly oversupply (~2.0 g/kg) and combine sources.

## Carb quality

"Slow" vs "fast" carbs matter less than people think. Within a balanced diet:
- Around training: fast-digesting carbs (rice, white potato, banana) work well.
- At meals away from training: complex carbs with fiber (oats, brown rice, lentils) for steadier energy and gut health.

Total fiber intake matters more for long-term health than which "type" of carbs you eat. Aim for 25–35 g/day from whole-food sources.

## Fat types

- **Saturated fat:** fine in moderation. Heavy dietary patterns high in saturated fat from processed sources are associated with worse health outcomes; from whole food sources (dairy, meat, eggs) the picture is more complex.
- **Monounsaturated:** olive oil, avocados, nuts. Strongly correlated with positive health outcomes.
- **Polyunsaturated:** further split into omega-3 (fish, walnuts, flax) and omega-6 (most vegetable oils). Most Western diets have plenty of omega-6 and not enough omega-3.

For most lifters: most fat from whole food sources (olive oil, avocados, nuts, fatty fish), don't fear saturated fat from minimally processed sources, and aim for 1–2 servings of fatty fish per week.

## What about timing?

Total daily intake matters most. Within that, timing protein evenly across 3–5 meals helps with muscle protein synthesis. Carbs around training help with performance. Fat is somewhat flexible.

Don't overthink it. Hit your totals, eat mostly whole foods, and your macros will sort themselves out.
