# Warm-up Protocols

Don't skip the warm-up. Don't drag it out either. Twenty minutes of foam rolling and "mobility flows" is just procrastinating the actual training. Here's a workable protocol.

## General warm-up (5 minutes)

Raise body temperature and get blood flowing. The goal is mild perspiration, not exhaustion.

- 3–5 minutes on a bike, rower, or jogging in place
- A few dynamic movements: leg swings, arm circles, hip circles

Skip the static stretching before lifting — it slightly reduces strength output and doesn't reduce injury risk in healthy lifters.

## Movement-specific warm-up

Before each main lift, work up to your working weight in 4–6 progressively heavier sets. The classic template:

For a 100 kg working set of 5 reps:
- Empty bar × 5–10 reps
- 40% × 5
- 55% × 5
- 70% × 3
- 80% × 2
- 90% × 1
- Working sets

The exact percentages don't matter. What matters is:
1. Each warm-up set is heavier than the last.
2. Reps drop as weight goes up.
3. You never get fatigued from warm-ups — they're priming, not training.

## Activation work

Some lifters benefit from low-load activation work before the main lift:

- **Before squat:** band pull-aparts, glute bridges
- **Before bench:** scapular wall slides, band pull-aparts
- **Before deadlift:** glute bridges, banded good mornings

These are 1–2 sets of 10–15 reps with light or zero load. Don't fatigue yourself.

## What to skip

- Static stretching pre-workout
- Foam rolling for 20 minutes
- Elaborate "movement prep" sequences from social media
- Lengthy mobility flows

If you have a specific mobility limitation, address it in a dedicated session or on rest days. Pre-workout time is for warming up the target movement, not for general mobility work.

## Cold-start warm-ups (low-temperature gyms)

If you train in a cold garage gym in winter:
- Extra 2–3 minutes of general cardio
- Higher-rep / lighter empty-bar work to drive blood into the working tissues
- Don't strip your warmups to "save time" — cold tissues are stiff tissues

## When to extend the warm-up

If your first working set feels harder than usual, you under-warmed-up. Add a heavier warm-up single before your first working set.

If it feels EASIER than usual, you might be ready for a heavier working set — note it and adjust next session.

## A note on rest between warm-up sets

Short rest (60–90s) is fine for early warm-ups. Move to full rest (2–3 min) before your heaviest warm-up sets and into the working sets.
