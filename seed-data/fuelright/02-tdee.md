# Calculating TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)

Reminder: I'm not a substitute for medical advice. If you have conditions affecting metabolism (thyroid, diabetes, PCOS, etc.), work with a clinician on your specific numbers.

## What TDEE is

Your TDEE is the total number of calories your body burns in a typical day. It's the foundation of any body composition plan — you can't intelligently choose a calorie target without an estimate.

TDEE has four components:

1. **BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate):** energy burned at rest, just keeping you alive. ~60–70% of TDEE.
2. **TEF (Thermic Effect of Food):** energy to digest food. ~10% of TDEE. Higher for protein, lower for fat.
3. **NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis):** fidgeting, walking, standing, all unintentional movement. Varies widely between people — can be 15% to 50% of TDEE.
4. **EAT (Exercise Activity Thermogenesis):** intentional exercise. Often the smallest component.

## Estimating BMR

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation is the most widely-accepted starting point:

**Men:** BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5
**Women:** BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161

Example: a 30-year-old man, 80 kg, 180 cm.
BMR = (10 × 80) + (6.25 × 180) − (5 × 30) + 5 = 800 + 1125 − 150 + 5 = 1780 kcal.

## Estimating TDEE

Multiply BMR by an activity factor:
- **Sedentary (desk job, little exercise):** ×1.2
- **Lightly active (1–3 light workouts/week):** ×1.375
- **Moderately active (3–5 workouts/week):** ×1.55
- **Very active (6–7 workouts/week or physical job):** ×1.725
- **Extremely active (heavy training + physical job):** ×1.9

Same 30-year-old, training 4x/week: TDEE ≈ 1780 × 1.55 ≈ 2760 kcal.

## The reality: it's an estimate

These equations are starting points, not ground truth. Two people of identical weight, height, and age can have TDEE differing by 300+ kcal. Why:

- **NEAT varies wildly.** A fidgety person can burn 400+ kcal/day more than a still one at the same weight.
- **Metabolic adaptation.** People who have dieted heavily often have suppressed metabolism for months or years after.
- **Body composition.** More muscle = higher BMR than what the equations predict.

## How to refine your estimate

The equations are starting points. The data is what your scale and tape measure tell you over 2–3 weeks.

1. Hit your calculated TDEE consistently for 2 weeks. Weigh daily, average per week.
2. If weight is stable: your true TDEE is close to your estimate.
3. If weight is rising at maintenance calories: TDEE is lower than estimated.
4. If weight is dropping at maintenance: TDEE is higher than estimated.

Adjust by ±100–200 kcal/day and observe for another 1–2 weeks.

## Common errors

- **Underestimating calorie intake.** Most people underestimate intake by 20–30%. The bigger sin than overestimating burn.
- **Trusting fitness tracker calorie estimates.** They're often 30%+ off, especially for strength training.
- **Reading scale fluctuations as fat change.** Day-to-day scale weight reflects water, glycogen, gut contents. Look at weekly averages.

## What about online calculators?

They all use variations of the equations above. Use one if you don't want to do math, but don't expect it to be more accurate than your own arithmetic.

## When to recalculate

Recalculate TDEE every 5–7 kg of body composition change or every 3 months, whichever comes first. As weight changes, BMR shifts. As you build muscle, BMR rises slightly. As you cut, BMR drops more than the equation predicts (metabolic adaptation).
