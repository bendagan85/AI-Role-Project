# Bench Press — Setup, Grip, and Bar Path

The bench is the lift most people overcomplicate. The fix is almost always to slow down, set up properly, and respect the bar path.

## The setup checklist

1. **Eye line under the bar.** When you lie back, your eyes should be directly under the bar. This tells you how far to scoot up the bench before unracking.
2. **Feet flat on the floor, planted hard.** Drive your heels down. The bench is a leg drive lift — your legs are part of the chain.
3. **Upper back tight, retract scapulae.** Pull your shoulder blades down and back. This creates a stable shelf and shortens the range of motion.
4. **Slight arch.** Not a powerlifting cathedral arch — just a natural arch that lifts the chest. The bar should land on a chest, not on your sternum.
5. **Glutes on the bench.** No bridging (illegal in powerlifting; doesn't matter for training but worth noting).
6. **Grip width.** Index finger one thumb-length outside the smooth ring is a good starting width. Wider = more chest, narrower = more triceps.

## Bar path

The bar travels in a slight diagonal — down to about the lower chest / upper abdomen, up and back to over the shoulders at lockout. NOT straight up and down.

If the bar comes straight down and up, you're either benching to the wrong point (touching too high) or you have weak triceps and the bar drifts forward at lockout.

## The two-second rule for stalling lifts

If your bench is stuck, slow the eccentric down. Two seconds on the way down, controlled, with the bar staying on its path. This builds the positions and stretch-reflex you need to drive heavier weights.

## Grip width tradeoffs

- **Wide grip:** Recruits more pec, shorter ROM, more shoulder stress over time.
- **Narrow grip:** More triceps, longer ROM, generally easier on shoulders.
- **Default:** Index finger at the inner ring. Adjust based on what you need to grow and what feels best on your shoulders.

## Foot position

Flat on the floor, planted, generating leg drive into the bench. The leg drive doesn't move the hips up off the bench — it shoves you back into the bench, anchoring your upper back.

Some lifters tuck feet way back under the hips for more arch. This is a powerlifting cue and adds risk for general training. Save it for max attempts if you're competing.

## Common errors

1. **Touching too high (sternum).** Move the touch point to the lower chest line.
2. **Elbows flared at 90°.** Tuck them to ~45°. Less shoulder stress, better leverage.
3. **Bouncing the bar off the chest.** Control the bottom. Brief pause if needed.
4. **Glute lift.** Drives sloppy form. Keep glutes down.
5. **No leg drive.** Most people leave 10–15% of their bench on the table by being lazy with feet.

## Pre-bench warm-up

Don't go into your working bench cold. Light band pull-aparts, scapular wall slides, and a few empty-bar reps to groove the position. The shoulder is the most fragile joint in this lift — earn the right to put weight on it.
