# Sleep, Stress, and Hunger Hormones

Disclaimer: I'm a dietitian, not a doctor. If sleep, mood, or appetite issues are persistent and severe, please see a clinician.

## The hormones that drive hunger

A simplified picture:

- **Ghrelin** — released by the stomach. Drives hunger. Rises before meals, drops after eating.
- **Leptin** — produced by fat cells. Signals "I'm full / have enough fuel" to the brain. Drops with weight loss.
- **Insulin** — from the pancreas. Released after meals. Among other things, signals satiety.
- **Cortisol** — stress hormone. Chronically high cortisol drives hunger and fat storage in the abdomen.
- **GLP-1 / PYY** — released by the gut after eating. Strong satiety signals.

You don't need to track these individually. You just need to know what disrupts them.

## What sleep deprivation does

A single night of bad sleep (< 5 hours):
- Ghrelin rises 15–20% (more hunger)
- Leptin drops 10–18% (less satiety)
- Insulin sensitivity drops 30–40%
- Cravings for high-carb, high-fat foods rise

Multiple consecutive nights of bad sleep magnify this. Studies show people sleep-deprived for a week eat 300–600 kcal more per day on average without feeling more full.

## The cascade

Poor sleep → higher cortisol + ghrelin → more hunger + bad food choices → more snacking + less protein → less satiety → more eating → weight gain or stalled fat loss.

This is why "I'm doing everything right but can't lose weight" often has sleep as the hidden variable.

## Practical sleep hygiene

The basics, in order of impact:

1. **Consistent bed/wake times** — even on weekends. Your circadian rhythm doesn't know what day it is.
2. **Dark, cool room** — 18–20 °C, blackout curtains.
3. **Caffeine cutoff 8 hours before bed.**
4. **No alcohol within 3 hours of bed** — it knocks you out but wrecks deep sleep.
5. **Reduce screens 30 min before bed** — or at minimum dim them and use night mode.
6. **Last big meal at least 2 hours before bed.**
7. **Bedtime routine** — anything that signals "wind down."

## Stress

Chronic stress is harder to fix than acute. Acute stress (a hard week at work) usually self-corrects when the trigger passes. Chronic stress (year-round work pressure, financial issues, relationship strain) requires intervention.

Practical effects on nutrition:
- Cravings, especially for carbs and sweets
- Reduced satiety signaling (you eat past full)
- Higher cortisol shifts fat storage toward the abdomen
- Reduced sleep quality (compounding the cycle)

What helps:
- Regular exercise (cardio especially)
- Sleep hygiene (above)
- Time outdoors
- Therapy or counseling for persistent stressors
- Practices like meditation, yoga, breathwork — modest effects but consistent

## Hunger management practical tools

For when you're hungry and need to eat well:

**Protein at every meal.** 30+g per meal is the most satiating macro.

**Volume eating.** Vegetables, broth-based soups, leafy salads — they fill the stomach with few calories.

**Fiber.** 25–35g/day from whole foods. Slows digestion, supports gut hormones (GLP-1, PYY).

**Avoid eating in a stressed or distracted state.** Bare minimum, don't eat while scrolling your phone. Your satiety signals fire weaker when distracted.

**Don't skip meals during a stressed week.** Most people overeat at the next meal to compensate.

## The "I'm always hungry on a diet" issue

If you're chronically hungry on a moderate cut, the usual culprits:
1. **Protein too low.** Bump to 2.0+ g/kg.
2. **Sleep too short.** Hunger hormones are dysregulated.
3. **Fat too low.** Below 0.5 g/kg, you lose satiety.
4. **Fiber too low.** Below 20g/day, gut hormones don't fire well.
5. **Hidden calories from drinks.** Liquids don't satiate the way solids do.

Fix these before assuming you need a bigger calorie target.

## Late-night eating

Calories at midnight don't store differently than calories at noon. But late-night eating tends to be:
- Eaten quickly while watching TV (poor satiety)
- Higher in processed snack foods
- Adding to total calories rather than replacing earlier ones
- Disrupting sleep if heavy

Practically, eating heavy 2+ hours before bed is fine. Eating heavy in bed is not.

## A note on emotional eating

Most people emotionally eat sometimes. Occasional comfort eating around stress isn't a crisis. Patterned emotional eating that derails goals — that benefits from a therapist's help, not another diet plan.
