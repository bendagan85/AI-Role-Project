# Recovery and Sleep for Lifters

You don't get stronger in the gym. You get stronger between sessions, while you recover. The single highest-leverage recovery variable is sleep — by a wide margin.

## Sleep

7–9 hours of decent sleep. That's the recommendation. For lifters in a growth phase or doing high-volume work, 8+ hours is closer to optimal.

What "decent" means:
- Consistent bed/wake times (yes, even on weekends)
- Dark, cool room (18–20 °C)
- No screens for 30 minutes before bed (or use night mode + dim lighting)
- Caffeine cutoff 8 hours before bed (so 2 PM cutoff if you sleep at 10 PM)

You can't out-train bad sleep. If you're chronically getting 5–6 hours, no amount of program tweaking will fix the resulting performance and recovery deficits.

## What sleep does

- **Anabolic hormones (testosterone, growth hormone)** are released in highest concentrations during deep sleep.
- **Glycogen replenishment** and muscle protein synthesis run heavily during sleep.
- **Nervous system recovery** is largely a sleep process. Lifting is mostly a CNS sport — without recovery, your second hard session of the week is at half the quality.

## Nutrition for recovery

This intersects with the FuelRight side. The basics:

- Hit your daily protein target (1.6–2.2 g/kg).
- Don't run a steep deficit during heavy training phases. Maintenance or small surplus is best for strength gain.
- Carbs around training and in the evening meal both support recovery.

If you're chronically underfed, every other recovery strategy is a band-aid.

## Active recovery

On non-lifting days:
- Light walking (30–60 minutes) — promotes blood flow, doesn't tax recovery.
- Easy cycling or rowing — good for general aerobic base.
- Mobility work on problem areas — yes, NOT general stretching.

What to avoid on rest days:
- High-intensity cardio for the legs the day before squats / deadlifts.
- Long hikes or runs the day before heavy lower-body work.

## Stress

Non-training stress is invisible to most lifters but eats into recovery just like training does. Big work deadlines, sleep disruption, life crises — all reduce your recovery capacity.

Practical implications:
- During stressful life weeks, treat training as if you've added an extra session.
- Reduce volume, not intensity. Half the sets, same load. Or scrap the deload-able accessories entirely.
- If sleep is bad for 3+ nights, drop a session.

## Hydration and recovery

Mild chronic dehydration impairs everything from performance to digestion to sleep quality. Default: 30–35 mL of fluid per kg of bodyweight per day, more if you sweat heavily.

You don't need to be precise. Check your urine color — pale straw is the target. Dark yellow means you're behind.

## Saunas, ice baths, massage, etc.

Effects on actual recovery are modest. They can help subjectively (you feel better), which has value. But:

- Cold plunge / ice bath in the 6 hours after lifting can BLUNT the hypertrophy response. Save it for non-training days.
- Sauna 3–4x per week has small cardiovascular benefits.
- Massage probably helps soreness recover but doesn't change underlying adaptation.

These are bonuses. Sleep is the main event.

## When recovery is failing

Symptoms of under-recovery:
- Stagnant or regressing performance for 2+ weeks
- Persistent joint or tendon discomfort
- Resting heart rate elevated 5+ bpm above baseline
- Poor sleep quality despite enough sleep duration
- Low motivation, irritability

The fix is almost always: more food, more sleep, less training stress. Not new supplements.
