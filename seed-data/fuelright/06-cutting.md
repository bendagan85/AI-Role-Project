# Cutting Strategies — Losing Fat Without Losing Your Mind

General nutrition guidance. If you have a complex relationship with food, an eating disorder history, or specific medical conditions, please work with a clinician — not a generic plan.

## What a "cut" actually is

A calorie deficit sustained long enough to lose body fat. The body's response over time:
1. Loses water and glycogen (first 1–2 weeks)
2. Loses fat (weeks 2+)
3. Loses some muscle (unavoidable but minimizable)
4. Drops metabolic rate (modest, but real)
5. Hormones adjust — leptin drops, ghrelin rises, NEAT drops, thyroid output dips slightly

The longer the cut, the bigger these adaptations. So most cuts work best when you set a clear endpoint.

## How big a deficit?

For most people:
- **Aggressive cut:** ~25% below TDEE. Faster, but harder on training and adherence.
- **Moderate cut:** 15–20% below TDEE. The sweet spot for most lifters.
- **Slow cut:** 10% below TDEE. Easy to maintain, slower results.

A 75 kg adult with TDEE of 2600 might cut at:
- Aggressive: 1950 kcal (650 deficit)
- Moderate: 2100 kcal (500 deficit)
- Slow: 2350 kcal (250 deficit)

Most people overshoot the aggressiveness, blow up the deficit, and either burn out or rebound. Moderate is the right default.

## Macros during a cut

- **Protein: HIGHER than usual.** 2.0–2.4 g/kg. Protects muscle, increases satiety.
- **Fat: minimum 0.6 g/kg.** Don't go below 0.5 g/kg long-term — hormones suffer.
- **Carbs: whatever's left.** Don't fear carbs during a cut — they support training quality.

Example for a 75 kg adult at 2100 kcal:
- Protein: 165g (660 kcal, 31%)
- Fat: 70g (630 kcal, 30%)
- Carbs: 200g (800 kcal, 38%)

## How fast should you lose weight?

A reasonable target: **0.5–1.0% of bodyweight per week**. For a 75 kg adult that's 0.4–0.75 kg/week.

Faster than 1% per week increases muscle loss. Slower than 0.5% per week stretches the cut longer than necessary and rarely beats moderate-paced cuts for muscle retention.

Track weekly averages, not daily weights. Day-to-day fluctuations are mostly water.

## Training during a cut

- **Keep training intensity high.** Don't drop weights to chase higher reps.
- **Reduce volume modestly** (10–20%) — you don't have the recovery you used to.
- **Maintain the main lifts.** Strength loss during a well-run cut should be minimal (5–10% at most).
- **Skip "high-rep cardio circuits."** They don't burn meaningful additional fat and they wreck recovery.

## Common mistakes

1. **Going too aggressive too soon.** Burnout is the #1 cut killer. Start moderate, accelerate only if needed.
2. **Cutting protein when you cut calories.** Wrong way around — protein goes UP during a cut.
3. **Eliminating an entire macro.** "No carb" or "no fat" diets work because they reduce calories. They have no special advantage over balanced cuts.
4. **Daily weigh-ins driving panic.** Use weekly averages.
5. **No diet breaks.** Long cuts (8+ weeks) benefit from a 1-week diet break at maintenance every 6–8 weeks. Sanity, hormones, and adherence all improve.

## Hunger management

Volume eating: dense protein + vegetables + complex carbs feel filling at fewer calories than fast-food-style meals.

Examples of high-volume, low-calorie protein vehicles:
- 200g cottage cheese: ~150 kcal, 25g protein
- 4 egg whites + 1 whole egg: ~145 kcal, 22g protein
- 150g lean ground turkey breast: ~150 kcal, 32g protein

## When to stop the cut

End the cut when:
- You've reached your goal body composition
- You've been cutting 10+ weeks straight — diet break time
- Performance is dropping consistently for 2+ weeks
- Sleep, mood, or energy crashes

After the cut, transition to maintenance for 2–4 weeks before any new phase. Bump calories by ~250 kcal/day every 4–5 days back to maintenance. Don't go straight from deep cut to surplus — that's a recipe for fast weight regain.

## Practical tips that work

- Most meals from whole foods
- 4 meals per day, evenly spaced
- Caffeine in the morning, water all day
- Plan the week's meals on Sundays
- Don't keep trigger foods at home you can't moderate
