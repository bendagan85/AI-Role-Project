# Deload Weeks

A deload is a planned, structured reduction in training stress to let the body finish adapting to a hard training block. Done right, deloads come back stronger. Done wrong (or skipped), you grind into stagnation and overtraining.

## When to deload

Three signals, any of which justifies a deload:

1. **Scheduled:** Every 4–8 weeks of hard training. Default to every 6 weeks if you're not sure.
2. **Performance:** Working sets feeling harder at the same load for 2+ sessions in a row.
3. **Recovery:** Sleep is bad, motivation is low, joints feel inflamed, and you haven't changed nutrition or stress.

If two or more of those overlap, deload now.

## How to deload

Two reasonable approaches:

### The classic 50–60% deload

Drop working loads to 50–60% of normal. Keep reps and sets similar. Sessions feel easy but you preserve the movement patterns and frequency.

Example: if you're squatting 100 kg for 3x5 working, deload week is 55 kg for 3x5. Same number of sets, same reps, much lower load.

### Volume cut

Keep loads similar (drop 10%) but cut volume in half. From 4 working sets to 2. From 5 reps to 3.

Useful when you want to maintain neural "feel" of heavy weight but reduce total work.

## What NOT to deload

Don't take a full week off most of the time. Complete rest is sometimes useful (post-meet, post-illness), but for a routine deload, keep moving. Lighter sessions promote blood flow and recovery better than total rest.

Don't deload your accessories while keeping the main lifts heavy. The point is total stress reduction.

## Programmed vs unprogrammed deloads

If your program prescribes a deload week, take it as written. Most intermediate templates (5/3/1, GZCL, conjugate) build them in.

If you're on a program without prescribed deloads (Starting Strength, simple linear progression), insert one when symptoms appear. Don't wait for catastrophic failure.

## After the deload

The first session back at normal weight should feel almost too easy. That's the point — you should be coming back fresh. If your first week back already feels hard, your deload wasn't long enough.

Common mistake: trying to "make up" for the deload by adding extra volume the week after. Don't. Trust the process. The deload's job is to let adaptation catch up, not to extract additional gains.

## Mini-deloads

For experienced lifters, scattered "mini-deloads" — single low-volume days — can work better than full deload weeks. Skip the heavy set, do volume work only, then come back full force the next session.

This requires honest self-assessment. Most lifters err on the side of pushing through. Lean toward deloading more often than you think you need.

## Deloads and life stress

Big work deadline this week? Travel? Bad sleep? Treat that week as a deload by default, even if you weren't scheduled for one. Training is a stressor. Pile it on top of other stressors and recovery drops, performance drops, injury risk climbs.
