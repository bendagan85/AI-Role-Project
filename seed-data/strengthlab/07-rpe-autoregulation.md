# RPE and Autoregulation

RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) is one of the most useful tools in strength training. It's a way to grade how hard a set was on a 1–10 scale, where 10 means "no more reps possible." Used well, it lets you adjust load day to day based on how you actually feel.

## The scale (Mike Tuchscherer's version)

- **RPE 10:** Maximum effort. No more reps possible.
- **RPE 9:** Could have done one more rep.
- **RPE 8:** Could have done two more reps.
- **RPE 7:** Could have done three more reps.
- **RPE 6:** Could have done four more reps. (Warm-up territory.)
- **RPE 5 or less:** Light work, not training.

Most working sets in a program target RPE 7–9. Pure strength work lives in the 7–8 range for sets of 1–5 reps. Hypertrophy work lives in the 8–9 range for 6–12 reps.

## Why use RPE instead of %1RM?

Two reasons:

1. **Your max changes day to day.** Sleep, stress, food, time of day — your true max can swing 5–10% from one day to the next. A program that prescribes 85% of 1RM ignores this.
2. **Your max is rarely tested.** Most lifters don't actually max out, so their "%1RM" is a guess. RPE doesn't care about your max — it cares about effort.

RPE lets you adjust on the fly: if 100 kg felt like RPE 9 today but the program expected RPE 7, the right move is to drop the weight, not to grind through three more sets at the same load.

## Common mistakes

1. **Calling every set RPE 8 just because it's the prescribed RPE.** Be honest. If three reps stayed in the tank, it's an RPE 7.
2. **Always working at RPE 9–10.** This is "max-out culture." It feels productive but burns out your nervous system and stalls progress. Most working sets should be RPE 7–8.
3. **Ignoring downward signals.** If your warm-ups feel sluggish and your first working set feels like an RPE 9 at a weight that was RPE 7 last week, the right answer is to back off, not push through.

## RPE for novices

For your first few months, RPE is hard to use because you don't yet have an internal model of "two more reps in the tank." Linear progression at fixed loads works fine. Start integrating RPE around month 3–6 once you have a feel for it.

## RPE-based load drops

A useful pattern: if your top set at the prescribed RPE was heavier than expected, drop 10% for your backoff sets. If it was lighter than expected, hold the same weight for the backoff sets. This naturally adjusts daily volume to readiness.

## When to ignore RPE

For competition prep, you eventually need to hit specific weights regardless of how they feel — because on meet day, the bar is loaded with a specific number. So in the last 4–6 weeks of a peaking block, RPE matters less and prescribed loads matter more.

For general training, RPE is almost always the better lever.
