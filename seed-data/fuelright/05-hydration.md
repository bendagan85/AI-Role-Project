# Hydration

General nutrition guidance, not medical advice.

## Daily baseline

A reasonable starting point: **30–35 mL of fluid per kg of bodyweight per day**.

For a 75 kg adult: 2.25–2.6 L of fluid. For a 90 kg adult: 2.7–3.15 L. This includes ALL fluids — water, tea, coffee, soup, milk, fruit juice. Solid foods also contribute ~20% of daily fluid in a typical mixed diet.

This is for a moderate-temperature, moderately-active lifestyle. Add more if:
- You sweat heavily during training (+500 mL to 1 L per training session)
- You live in a hot climate (+15–20% baseline)
- You drink a lot of coffee or alcohol (mild diuretic effect)

## How to know you're hydrated

The simplest check is urine color throughout the day:

- Pale straw or light yellow = well hydrated
- Bright yellow = on the edge
- Dark yellow / amber = under-hydrated, drink more
- Almost clear = you may be over-hydrated

A second check: thirst. Most adults are mildly dehydrated chronically because they ignore thirst. If you're thirsty, drink. If you're not, you don't NEED to chug.

## Hydration around training

**Pre-workout:** 500 mL of water in the 1–2 hours before you train. Don't chug 1 liter in the 20 minutes before lifting — it'll slosh in your stomach.

**During:** sip throughout. For sessions under 60 minutes at moderate intensity, water is enough. For longer sessions or higher intensity, electrolytes matter.

**Post-workout:** weigh yourself before and after training (no clothes, accurate scale). For every 1 kg you lost, drink 1.5 L back over the next few hours.

## Electrolytes

The major ones that matter for active people:

| Electrolyte | Daily target | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Sodium | 1500–4000 mg | Muscle contraction, blood pressure, hydration |
| Potassium | 3000–5000 mg | Heart function, muscle contraction, fluid balance |
| Magnesium | 300–450 mg | 300+ enzyme reactions, sleep quality, cramp prevention |

For most people eating mostly whole foods, magnesium and potassium come from the diet. Sodium can be tricky — many active people who avoid processed food are UNDER-sodium, especially during the summer or long training sessions.

## Sports drinks vs water

Save sports drinks for sessions over 75 minutes or sessions in heat. For shorter sessions, water plus a balanced meal afterward covers it.

If you're doing back-to-back hard sessions, a basic electrolyte drink (low-sugar) between can help. Skip the brightly-colored 30g-of-sugar versions for general training.

## Can you drink too much?

Yes — hyponatremia is real and dangerous. It happens when you drink large amounts of plain water and dilute your blood sodium. Most common in endurance athletes doing 4+ hour events.

For typical gym training, you'd have to actively try to over-hydrate to cause problems. Sipping naturally throughout the day and during training is safe.

## What about coffee dehydrating you?

The diuretic effect of caffeine is real but small at typical intakes (1–4 cups per day). The net hydration impact of a normal coffee is still positive — you're getting more water than you're losing.

Caffeine intake above ~500 mg/day can shift this balance slightly. But coffee doesn't "dehydrate" you in any practical sense.

## Sodium during cutting

When you reduce calories, you typically reduce sodium too (less processed food, smaller meal sizes). For some people this drops sodium intake too far. Symptoms:
- Mid-workout fatigue
- Lightheadedness when standing
- Cramps
- Brain fog

Fix: add a pinch (about 1g) of table salt to your water bottle, or include a few salty whole foods (broth, olives, salted nuts) daily.

## Practical default

If you train hard 3–5x per week:
- 30 mL/kg water as a baseline
- +500–1000 mL on training days
- A pinch of salt in pre-workout water during summer or long sessions
- Watch urine color; adjust
