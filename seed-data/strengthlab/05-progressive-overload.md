# Progressive Overload — Theory and Practice

If you're not progressively challenging your muscles, you're not training. You're just exercising. Progressive overload is the single principle that separates training from going through the motions.

## What "overload" actually means

Overload is any factor that pushes the working capacity above what your body has adapted to. The most common levers, in order of usefulness:

1. **Add weight.** Most direct. Same reps, more load.
2. **Add reps.** Same weight, more reps. Useful when you've stalled on weight.
3. **Add sets.** More total volume at the same load and reps.
4. **Improve technique.** A more efficient lift means the same load is harder for the working muscles in the right way.
5. **Reduce rest.** Same load, less recovery between sets. Increases metabolic stress.

For pure strength, levers 1 and 2 do almost all the work. For hypertrophy, sets, reps, and proximity-to-failure matter more.

## The double progression model

Most intermediate lifters get the most mileage out of double progression:

1. Set a rep range, e.g., 5–8 reps.
2. Work at a weight where you can do 5 reps with good form, at RPE 8.
3. Each week, try to add a rep at the same weight.
4. When you hit the top of the range (8 reps) at RPE 8, add 2.5–5 kg next session and start over at the bottom (5 reps).

Repeat. This works for almost any lift and almost any lifter for years.

## When linear progression dies

For novices, you can add weight every session for 8–16 weeks. Eventually that stops working. Symptoms:

- You miss reps two sessions in a row at the same weight after deloading.
- Sleep, nutrition, and recovery are all in order.
- You've been on the same program for 3+ months.

When that happens, move to weekly progression (add 2.5 kg per week), or to a structured intermediate template (5/3/1, Texas Method, GZCL).

## Volume vs intensity vs frequency

Three knobs:
- **Volume:** total work done (sets × reps × weight)
- **Intensity:** load relative to your max (%1RM or RPE)
- **Frequency:** how often you train a movement

Most lifters undertrain frequency. Squatting once a week is leaving gains on the table. Two to three squat sessions per week, with one heavy and one or two lighter or higher-rep, drives more progress than a single heroic Monday session.

## Plateaus and what to do

If you've stalled for 3–4 weeks on a lift, the fix is usually NOT more weight. Try one of:

- **Add a variation day:** front squat or pause squat once a week to address weak ranges.
- **Reduce frequency briefly:** sometimes a stalled lift just needs more recovery.
- **Reset and rebuild:** drop the working weight 10–15% and rebuild over 6 weeks.
- **Check the basics:** sleep, food, stress. Most plateaus are recovery problems, not training problems.

## What not to do

- Don't change programs every two weeks. Give a template at least 8 weeks before judging it.
- Don't grind every set. Leave a rep or two in the tank on most working sets. Push closer to failure on the last set only.
- Don't ignore form for the sake of progression. "Adding weight" through worse form is just adding injury risk.
